Italian Language

I have taught Italian for nearly a decade, on two continents and at every level — from absolute beginners to candidates preparing for national examinations. I am a writer as well as a teacher, a habit that has shaped how I think about the language and how I teach it.

Teaching

My teaching began in 2016, far from home: I was a lettore (language instructor) at Peking University (北京大学) in Beijing, teaching both modern and historical Italian to Chinese undergraduates. It is a strange and useful thing to teach your own language to people who share almost none of your assumptions about it — you learn very quickly which of your explanations actually hold up, and which ones you had only ever repeated out of habit.

I have taught ever since, both in person and online, to students of all ages and backgrounds across the world. Some come to Italian out of love for the literature or the country, some for family reasons, some because their work or their studies require it. The starting points differ; the work of getting from one to the next is much the same.

At Princeton I have taught in the Department of History and in the Department of East Asian Studies, and this coming fall semester I will be teaching in the Department of Comparative Literature. I am also a certified instructor through Princeton's Teaching Transcript, the university's program of formal training and certification in university teaching.

In 2020 and 2021 I was the official voice of the AP Italian examination (Advanced Placement) in the United States — the recorded voice that candidates across the country heard in the listening sections of the exam.

Writing

I write as well as teach, and the two feed each other. My work has appeared in some of Italy's most important literary magazines, and I have won one of Italy's most respected literary prizes.
